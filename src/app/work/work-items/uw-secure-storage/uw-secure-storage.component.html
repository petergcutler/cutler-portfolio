Secure Storage for Tent Cities

Designing a user-centered solution to help homeless Seattle residents store their possessions securely and sustainably
  • UX Research
  • Research Planning
  • Information Architecture
  • Wireframing
  • Prototyping

In September 2017, I formed a group with three other graduate students to find a problem and design a user-centered solution. We were all motivated to work on a project that was socially-responsible, topical, and relevant to our local region.

We decided to work on an issue that impacts thousands of Seattle's citizens: design a user-centered solution to help homeless Seattle residents store their possessions securely and sustainably.

Opportunity Space and Scope

At the outset of the project, our opportunity space was broad: we wanted to look at problems for homelessness residents of Seattle. In order to find the right issue to examine, we began a literature review and reached out to experts for interviews. Some of our findings at this time included:

  • We each combed dozens of in-depth investigative reports. We created many notes like this one, aggregating and highlighting opportunities that we could further investigate.
  • We found many experts willing to speak with us. I began a conversation with Barbara Poppe, an appointee by President Obama to the U.S. Interagency Council on Homelessness. She helped validate and confirm our trajectory early on in the project.
  • With such an open ended challenge ahead, our lit review and semi-structured interviews helped us understand how a narrow scope could help us achieve tangible results.

As our group reviewed our findings and interviews, we decided to focus on designing sustainable storage solutions for Seattle's homeless populations. We chose this opportunity space because it was small enough in scope for us to explore, but large enough that it could provide benefits and peace of mind to many users:

  • With their possessions safe, users can travel more freely
  • Meet about social services with social workers and others
  • Travel to explore employment opportunities
  • Focus on ongoing tasks without worrying about loss prevention

Understanding the Domain

With a narrower focus in mind, our research continued — we would need to understand this complex system better in order to know our constraints and opportunities. We first sought to understand what audiences were involved, and what stakeholder objectives would define a viable solution.

Types of Homeless Housing Projects

Seattle presents many distinct populations of homeless residents who are relying on a variety of housing programs for support.

These housing programs differ across the types of shelter, staff support, amenities, and other factors of care provided. Understanding this complex network of housing programs was a critical first step to designing a viable solution.

Financial Resources Available

Beyond existing housing programs, we also wanted to understand the financial resources that the city puts towards homelessness. We investigated how budgetary resources are organized in Seattle.

The resources here, like in many cities, are scarce. But understanding the financial context was critical to designing a realistic solution.

A Philosophy of Housing First

Through literature review and semi-structured interviews, we came to align ourselves with a budgetary concept of housing first — the idea that the overwhelming majority of city funds should be put towards the creation of permanent housing solutions rather than stop-gap measures.

As we progressed, we often asked ourselves, "is this solution worth more than a single permanent home?" Prioritizing "housing first," we knew our solution would have to use resources responsibly — either through a low cost or through an easy method of scaling the resources required.

Conducting a Competitive Analysis

As we researched existing solutions across the domain, we found that most of the designed solutions didn't account for the wide variety of housing programs that exist. Existing solutions also frequently employed costly materials or failed to align with the resource scarcity of the system.

The H.O.P.E. lockers, shown above, cost $10,000 to build. The funds were raised through an indiegogo campaign. Another storage pilot program in Denver, shown below, cost $100,000 to initiate, and included a high upkeep cost each month onward.

Based on the narrow scope of existing solutions, we saw an opportunity for a cheaper, more sustainable, community-oriented system.

Surveying Shelter Residents

In order to build an understanding of our potential design's users, we began planning outreach to residents of local shelters. We crafted two surveys — one for shelter administrators and one for shelter residents — to get a sense of a variety of stakeholders for a storage-oriented project. Some of our questions for stakeholders included:

  • How long do residents leave belongings unattended?
  • How do you ensure security?
  • How do you manage access?
  • How much space is available at your shelter for storage?

And for residents:

  • Do you co-habitate with others?
  • Would you be comfortable sharing storage with your group?
  • Are you comfortable having fellow community members watch over lockers?
  • What storage size do you require?

In addition to the above questions, we also asked a number of questions about the level of trust within the community, the logistics and operations, and how communication is handled between residents in different scenarios.

Visiting and Interviewing

Our group had a particular interest in Seattle's tent cities. These communities in particular are transitional, highly vulnerable, and were heavily publicized during the mayoral elections. To gain an understanding of the needs of residents of transitional housing, we visited Nickelsville Ballard to tour and interview.

Our guide, Jamie, gave us a tour and answered our questions in an interview. From our interview with Jamie, we gained a much deeper understanding of the relationship residents have with their belongings and storage. Some of our takeaways included:

  • Residents have significant needs for privacy, which extends to their storage.
  • Transitional communities must be able to move on short notice due to raids or other relocation needs. Residents must be able to bring both their goods and the means to store them.
  • Jamie reiterated what our surveys found — residents have a lot of possessions, often over 500 or 1000 liters.
  • There is often room for more storage, but no structure to house goods.

Early Design Ideas from the Field

One of my design ideas came from this field visit — from seeing the kind of structural supports depicted in the last three picture above. Many of the structures in the Nickelsville Ballard camp were fabricated from a mix of skeletal supports (the kind of plastic, PVC type material shown), and a simple waterproof exoskeleton, like a standard tarp. I knew I wanted to explore this kind of structural, component-based system more.

Synthesizing Personas and Early Design

Looking at our findings from the field visit and from our survey responses, we created a set of personas to represent the most common findings from our research. Rather than editorialize approximate stories for our personas, we based the descriptions and metadata of each persona on the most common, real tropes we found in our research data.

With a more codified understanding of our diverse users, we began to design simple, sketch-based concepts for our early solutions. I developed three sketches that each explored ways to add storage at a minimal cost, using commonly available materials.

  • Use cheap, high-volume plastic footlockers as a foundation for a larger array of storage.
  • Work with the city to reappropriate large recycling containers to be used as lockers.
  • Finally, taking the idea of the mixed materials found around Nickelsville, create a library of "component-based" storage solutions that users can browse to create a storage solution from the materials they have on hand.

Iterating on a Single Design

As we met as a team and discussed our sketches, we decided to pursue the modular, component-based solution shown above. We liked the idea of a library that users could browse to find different structures they could build with any range of available materials. It aligned with our survey findings regarding the changing needs of scale, features, and cost that our users wanted. It also had a clear basis in the materials we saw in our field visit.

With a solution in mind, we had a lot of work to do to iterate and expand on the design. Over the next two weeks, we explored implementation, prototypes, and a representative information architecture for how such a system would work. We presented our design in a comprehensive brief.
