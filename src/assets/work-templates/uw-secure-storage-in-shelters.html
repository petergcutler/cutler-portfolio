Secure Storage for Tent Cities
Designing a user-centered solution to help homeless Seattle residents store their possessions securely and sustainably.

In September 2017, I joined the University of Washington's HCDE program, and began my first core class: a foundational user-centered design course. I formed a group with three others to find a problem and design a user-centered solution. As we met and began to discuss the task, we found we were all motivated to choose a problem that was socially-responsible, current, under-investigated, and relevant to our local region.

We selected a project that could resolve social, economic, and health hazards for thousands of Seattle's citizens: design a user-centered solution to help homeless Seattle residents store their possessions securely and sustainably.

Opportunity Space and Scope

At the outset of the project, our opportunity space was broad: we wanted to look at solutions that would in some way address homelessness in Seattle. So our first task was to narrow our scope. To do so, we began a brief literature review.

Seattle's effort to address homelessness is significant - so much so that in late 2017, it was a primary topic of the ongoing mayoral race between Jenny Durkan and Cary Moon. Fortunately for us, this meant that it was also heavily spotlighted by local media. Our literature review focused on the long-form investigative pieces across Seattle's shelters and tent cities. In the course of my review, I put together many notes like this one, aggregating and highlighting key opportunities that we could further investigate.

During our lit review, we also began outreach to experts in the field. I wrote to Barbara Poppe, an appointee by President Obama to the U.S. Interagency Council on Homelessness. I began a discussion with her about our group's issue area and intent that helped inform our focus. She helped to validate and confirm our trajectory early on in the project. We also contacted University of Washington professors in the school of public health to hold informal interviews about our progress.

As our group reviewed our findings and interviews, we decided to focus on designing sustainable storage solutions for Seattle's homeless populations.

Understanding the Domain

Seattle presents many distinct populations of homeless residents who are relying on a variety of housing programs for support.

These housing programs differ across the types of shelter, staff support, amenities, and other factors of care provided. Understanding this complex network of housing programs was a critical first step to designing a viable solution.

Beyond existing housing programs, we also wanted to understand the financial resources that the city puts towards homelessness. We investigated how the budgetary resources are organized in Seattle. The resources here, like in many cities, are scarce. But understanding the financial context was critical to designing a realistic solution.

Through literature review and secondary-source interviews, we came to align ourselves with a budgetary concept of housing first - the idea that the overwhelming majority of city funds should be put towards the creation of permanent housing solutions rather than stop-gap measures.

As we progressed, we often asked ourselves, "is this solution worth more than a single permanent home?" Prioritizing "housing first," we knew our solution would have to use resources responsibly - either through a low cost or through an easy method of scaling the resources required.

Conducting a Competitive Analysis

As we researched existing solutions across the domain, we found that most of the designed solutions didn't account for the wide variety of housing programs that exist. Existing solutions also frequently employed costly materials or failed to align with the resource scarcity of the system.

The H.O.P.E. lockers, shown above, cost $10,000 to build. The funds were raised through an indiegogo campaign. Another storage pilot program in Denver, shown below, cost $100,000 to initiate, and included a high upkeep cost each month onward.

Based on the narrow scope of existing solutions, we saw an opportunity for a cheaper, more sustainable, community-oriented system.

Surveying Shelter Residents

In order to build an understanding of our potential design's users, we began planning outreach to residents of local shelters. We crafted two surveys - one for shelter administrators and one for shelter residents - to get a sense of a variety of stakeholders for a storage-oriented project. Some of our questions for stakeholders included:

  • How long do residents leave belongings unattended?
  • How do you ensure security?
  • How do you manage access?
  • How much space is available at your shelter for storage?

And for residents:

  • Do you co-habitate with others?
  • Would you be comfortable sharing storage with your group?
  • Are you comfortable having fellow community members watch over lockers?
  • What storage size do you require?

In addition to the above questions, we also asked a number of questions around the level of trust among the community, some methods of logistics, and how communication is handled between residents in different scenarios.

Visiting and Interviewing

Our group had a particular interest in Seattle's tent cities. These communities in particular are transitional, highly vulnerable, and were heavily publicized during the mayoral elections. To gain an understanding of the needs of residents of transitional housing, we visited Nickelsville Ballard to tour and interview.

Our guide, Jamie, provided us a tour and answered our questions in an interview. From our interview with Jamie, we gained a much deeper understanding of the relationship residents have with their belongings and storage. Some of our takeaways included:

  • Residents have significant needs for privacy, which extends to their storage.
  • Transitional communities must be able to move on short notice due to raids or other relocation needs. Residents must be able to bring both their goods and the means to store them.
  • Jamie reiterated what our surveys found - residents have a lot of possessions, often over 500 or 1000 liters.
  • There is often room for more storage, but no structure to house goods.
