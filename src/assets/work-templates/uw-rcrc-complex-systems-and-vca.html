Collaborative Research with the IFRC

Understanding a complex humanitarian system, and planning a human-centered intervention to improve vulnerability assessments

Through a unique partnership between the International Federation of Red Cross and Red Crescent Societies (IFRC) and the University of Washington, my class studying Complex Systems was able to collaboratively research ways to incorporate Human-Centered principles into the Vulnerability & Capacity Assessment (VCA) system.

The IFRC has been a leader in the development and application of participatory design systems, and the VCA system is just one such example. As experts on the meeting points of technology and human-centered research & design, we collaborated with many Red Cross teams to layout distinct opportunities to better integrate new technology into their participatory VCA approach.

Building Understanding

At the outset of this project, our first step was to recalibrate our understanding of our domain to align with humanitarian principles and with Universal Human Rights.

If you want to understand some of this task of recalibration, please take a look at an excellent, quick summary of contextual shifts involved. Designing with a humanitarian's outlook is not as intuitive as it sounds!

Simultaneously, we dug deeply into the notion of complex systems and system design, reading two texts that informed our understanding.

  • Streetlights & Shadows — Gary Klein's investigation into what it means to design in and for a complex, changing environment.
  • Information Ecology — Thomas Davenport's exploration into the human-centered basis of the information we share.

This period of research aided us in understanding, first, the domain of our clients at the IFRC, and in turn, how they support millions of individuals through the VCA system.

Some examples of the many take-aways from this period include:

  • The community comes first. While there are many stakeholders in the VCA system, it is the community that determines the plan and owns the results.
  • In this domain, there are absolutely no quid pro quos. All of our actions are in the service of the community. Furthermore, we cannot define what serves the community — only they can do that.
  • Communicating scope is the best way to mitigate the constant push and pull of changing variables in a large, complex system. Stakeholders, requirements, participants, and directives may constantly change, but by maintaining a shared sense of scope, the project can still succeed.
  • Systems don't succeed or fail; rather, they shift around the active stakeholders' competing objectives. As researchers, our first task is to understand the system and those influencing it.

It's impossible to communicate the complexity of this system in a single case study, but I hope the resources above can provide some important context. This research period helped me to understand how to approach a complex system and build understanding from many different perspectives at once.

Finding Opportunities

Our task in this class was not to design an intervention, but to plan one that would unfold over the ensuing year. Subsequent research groups would use our findings and our plan to progress from research into design. So having developed a nuanced understanding of the domain, our job was to outline research avenues that ensuing teams could explore.
