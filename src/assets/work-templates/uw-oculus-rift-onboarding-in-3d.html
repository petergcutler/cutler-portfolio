On-boarding with the Oculus Platform

Conducting a usability test to evaluate first-time user experiences with Oculus Touch and motion-controlled software.

In January 2018, I formed a group with three other graduate students to develop and conduct a usability test. We were interested in testing a product with a complex, novel interface — something that presented different questions and opportunities than traditional web applications.

We decided to work with VR, using the Oculus platform. We were interested in understanding how first-time users would grapple with this new interface — what hurdles they would identify during on-boarding, and how they would adapt and operate a software interface with no prior experience.

VR Applications

At the outset of the project, we decided to focus on two Oculus applications:

  • First Contact — the official on-boarding experience provided by Oculus
  • Tilt Brush — a 3d design application designed for VR

Why these applications? First, we wanted to test an interface-driven product, rather than a game. I had purchased the Rift when it launched, and had seen how 3d design applications were taking advantage of the VR medium. As a team, we explored Tilt Brush together and decided it was a great candidate.

We also determined that we couldn't drop users into a complex application like Tilt Brush without on-boarding. As we used First Contact, we found we were interested in understanding users' reception and comprehension of the application.

On the one hand, it's a very game-like experience, but on the other, it's responsible for imparting foundational context and direction to the user. We wanted to explore how the user perceived this experience, and how well it taught them.

Heuristic Evaluation

Our professor recommended that none of our teams seek out clients to work with for the purposes of the class. This gave us lattitude to explore based on our own interests (as well as other benefits), but it meant that we would have to come up with a way to determine our research objectives independently.

With no stakeholders to dictate our direction, we used a heuristic analysis of our applications to find worthwhile avenues to explore. Based on our evaluation, we found a wide array of possible problems to explore. We focused on a few out the outset:

  • Visibility of System Status: in First Contact, we found there to be few indications if we were making progress. In Tilt Brush, we found a lack of clarity around our current tool selections.
  • Consistency and standards: input expectations changed from app to app.
  • Error Prevention: both applications lead the user toward error, often through a lack of clear direction.
  • Aesthetic and Minimalist Design: menus and environments were visually noisy and sometimes overwhelming.
  • Help and Documentation: we found poor access to documentation within Tilt Brush.

Developing a Research Plan

Our next task was to codify our early findings into a concrete, clearly-scoped research plan.

We prioritized problems that were foundational, rather than those that could be attributed to "figuring out" emerging issues in VR. At this time, it's unrealistic that VR apps present a common, synchronous interface, for instance. We focused on education and on-boarding problems, and organized the following research questions:

  • What challenges do first-time users run into while using the Oculus platform?
  • How does ease of use for each app impact satisfaction for the entire experience?
  • How easily and successfully do first-time users interact with First Contact and Tilt Brush?

We also developed our research plan to outline how we would evaluate these questions.
